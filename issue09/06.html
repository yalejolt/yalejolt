Impermanence

from Wakan Rōeishū: A Collection of Japanese and Chinese Chanted Verse

translated by Ryan Hintzman

see the body: grass
  uprooted scattered
    on the banks
and say of life: a boat
  unmoored drifting
    by the shore

         —Luo Wei

year passes year, seasons turn
               and flowers return
seasons turn, year passes year
               and people don’t

         —Song Zhiwen

kingdoms perched atop snail
  horns: what’s there to seize?
a flint strike flashes: this body


         —Bai Juyi

  what lives must be
    snuffed out.
Buddha’s body: sandalwood
smoke; joy pours out and grief
  draws near—even
    gods face
      time’s decay

         —Ōe no Asatsuna

by morning,
  rosy cheeks: the world’s
yours—come evening,
  pale bones
    in a field, rotting.

         —Fujiwara no Yoshitaka

I behold the moon’s
  image reflected
    in autumn waves—
still bound and pulled
  by fame like spring
    flowers in dreams

         —Ōe no Asatsuna

ask:
  what’s the world
  like? dawn: a boat
    leaving a trace of
      foam-white waves

         —Monk Manzei

living in a world
  made of dreams or
    real, no, now alive
      dreaming—I can’t hold them
        apart, for I am and I am not.

         —poet unknown

held in my hands—the moon
  reflected in water   dwelling 
    for a moment, an image 
      of this world, where what is
        real, and not, can’t hold—apart.

         —Ki no Tsurayuki

on the highest branch perishing
  dew drops drip sprinkling
    roots, proof
      in our world some
        tarry, some go soon

         —Archbishop Henjō
