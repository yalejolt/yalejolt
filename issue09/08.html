To My Husband

by Hu Li, translated by Jingjing Xiao

Past the tile-roofed houses,
beyond the old city walls,
at the end of Shuijin Alley,
the scent of wine lingers in the road.

The bamboo bridge is short,
the sunset grows long,
and the water mills spin.
You dust your coat, then board the boat.

The spring river fills
with the tidewaters’ chill.
The lantern shadows sway,
and your eyes reflect light off the lake.

Under the flowers’ shade,
I opened an umbrella today,
waiting beside the bridge,
as officials in brocade came home.

  They told me,
    “Do not forget.”

I mistook the chimney woodsmoke
for you, pouring soup—
a bowl of wild herbs,
the aroma pouring over the room.

I take my longing,
let it stew, and drink.
It seeps into my bones
and pierces my lungs.

The lamplight against the wall—
it’s you, in a winter coat,
silhouette beneath the bright moon—
you, facing our window.

One white night,
I stare beyond the water,
burn red candles,
illuminate windflowers.

  Love, O love—
    when will you return?

March goes by, the fireworks cool.
Mid-July, the summer heat arrives.
October comes with freezing rain.
Winter snow blankets the ground.

A year ends,
swallows circle our roof.
Three autumns pass,
I grow thin.
Nine years go by,
my hair turns gray.
But your city remains
shrouded in mist and clouds.
Familiar songs and recitations
drift over the crowds.

  Love, O love—
    will you return?

I haven’t forgotten how
you counted the boats,
how you picked willow flowers
to adorn my clothes.

A thousand ships have passed,
but I still remember you
facing the sunset—
that broken expression.

  I haven’t.
    I haven’t. I haven’t forgotten—

Our rafters marked by the years,
    you marking the hours, slow and long.

That letter you sent home, each desolate
    paragraph made me wilt with longing,
            unable to move on.
